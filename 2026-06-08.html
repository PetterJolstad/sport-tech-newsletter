3 June 2026 · Officiating tech · FIFA

FIFA drops the semi-automated offside threshold to 10 cm and adds Lenovo-built 3D player avatars

Eight days before World Cup 26 kickoff, FIFA confirmed two material upgrades to the officiating stack. The semi-automated offside system (SAOT) will now flag an offside at > 10 cm of advantage, down from the 50 cm threshold used at Qatar 2022 and the Club World Cup 2025. The TRIONDA ball's 500 Hz IMU determines the kick-point in real time, while Lenovo has built individual 3D AI avatars for every one of the 1,248 players in the tournament — generated from a single one-second whole-body scan that captures limb lengths and segment dimensions — used to compute the player-position side of the offside line. FIFA's match-officials briefing this week also added an in-ear referee feed for SAOT calls.

For practitioners: this is the first World Cup where the limiting factor on an offside decision is genuinely the sensor stack and not the camera mesh — sub-10 cm classification implies sub-degree skeletal-joint reconstruction at video frame rates. The under-discussed practitioner angle is that every player in the tournament now has a publishable, FIFA-controlled 3D body scan on record. That dataset is large enough to recalibrate every off-the-shelf markerless-pose model in football within the next twelve months, and the rights to it will quietly become a recruitment chip.

🌱 Up-and-coming · prototype

Aerly Diagnostics demos a neck-worn wearable for exercise-induced laryngeal obstruction

Indiana University spin-out Aerly Diagnostics unveiled a prototype neck-worn sensor on 3 June that aims to objectively detect exercise-induced laryngeal obstruction (EILO) during training — a breathing disorder that mimics exercise-induced asthma and is routinely misdiagnosed in young athletes. The device combines an external acoustic microphone over the larynx with surface-EMG of strap-muscle activity and an IMU for breathing-cycle segmentation. Founder Rita Patel (IU School of Medicine, Speech-Language-Hearing) reports 100 % agreement with reference flexible laryngoscopy in the prototype clinical cohort.

Maturity: functioning prototype, no commercial release. Backing: a $3.7 M NIH award, plus a Winter 2026 spot in the IU LAB pre-accelerator (powered by gener8tor). Who's piloting: IU clinical pulmonology and ENT partners; field pilots with collegiate athletics not yet announced. What's genuinely new: EILO has no field-deployable test today — diagnosis means an exercising laryngoscopy in an ENT clinic, which is exactly the situation where the symptoms don't reproduce. A wearable that flags during real exertion would close a known practitioner gap, especially in adolescent middle-distance and rowing programmes. Skepticism: the 100 % accuracy headline is a tiny prototype cohort against a single comparator; the technical hard part — acoustic separation of breath-hold versus partial laryngeal closure on a moving runner in wind — is not solved by the sample size. Watch for a multi-site validation study before believing the headline.

Navigating the data processing maze: a systematic review of jump height calculations using force platforms

Eythorsdottir I., Gløersen Ø., Rice H., Solli G.S., Pedersen S., Sandbakk Ø. · open-access · current online issue June 2026

European Journal of Sport Science

The most practitioner-relevant force-plate paper of the year. Across 47 studies, the authors quantify how much jump height drifts purely as a function of the equation chosen (flight time, impulse–momentum, double-integration), the sampling frequency, the filter cut-off and the take-off threshold. The headline number: data-processing decisions alone can change reported jump height by up to ~26 %, and equation choice by ~15 cm. The non-headline: VALD, Hawkin and the open-source pipelines all default to different equations, so cross-system comparisons across your performance department are essentially unsupported unless you fix the processing chain.

Sports Engineering

A 90-minute 2022 World Cup match was processed with broadcast-derived player and ball tracking and benchmarked against TRACAB Gen 5 multi-camera optical tracking plus FIFA Data Collection Unit hand tags. Tech-talk

Markerless motion capture — computer vision finally eats the lab

Three years from being "a research toy"; this is the week it became officiating-grade. With FIFA's per-player avatar pipeline live in three days, the practitioner question is no longer "does it work" but "what should I use it for."

What it is

Markerless motion capture (MMC) is the umbrella term for systems that reconstruct an athlete's three-dimensional pose from one or more standard video cameras, with no markers, suits or skin-mounted sensors. The core machinery is a deep convolutional or transformer model trained to localise body landmarks (typically 20–50 joint centres) frame-by-frame; a second stage fits a kinematic skeleton, a third stage solves the inverse problem to produce joint angles, velocities and — increasingly — internal joint loads via musculoskeletal modelling. The category covers everything from a single-camera smartphone app to a forty-camera volumetric capture rig in a broadcast stadium.

Providers

  • Theia Markerless — Theia3D, the most extensively peer-validated platform in sport; the engine behind the Driveline Launchpad service for collegiate baseball (Ohio State, Florida State and others), with a 2026 expansion into integrated bat and ball tracking.
  • KinaTrax — multi-camera markerless capture used widely in MLB pitching biomechanics; lower-frame-rate but higher-volume than lab marker systems.
  • Hawk-Eye / Sony — broadcast-stadium multi-camera mesh now repurposed for skeletal pose in football (FIFA SAOT), basketball (NBA player + ball) and baseball (MLB ABS pipeline).
  • Lenovo — the new entrant; its FIFA World Cup 26 pipeline generates per-player 3D avatars from one-second whole-body scans, then fuses them with the Hawk-Eye camera mesh and the TRIONDA ball's IMU for offside calls.
  • Move 4D, Notch, Captury, Simi — broader research-grade and field-deployable systems.
  • Open-source — OpenPose, MMPose, OpenCap (Stanford), and the University of Bath's CAMERA open-access tooling for "biomechanics in the wild."

Specs that matter

  • Camera count: a single phone camera gives you 2D pose plus crude depth via temporal priors; reliable 3D needs at least four synchronised cameras; broadcast-grade joint reconstruction at sub-degree accuracy needs 8–12.
  • Frame rate: 50 Hz is fine for gait and posture; pitching biomechanics, sprint mechanics and bat tracking need 200–500 Hz, which is where smartphone tooling falls down.
  • Joint-angle accuracy versus marker-based gold standard: typically 2–5° RMSE for lower-limb angles in steady-state running, larger for transient movements (cutting, single-leg landings), and consistently worst for axial rotations.
  • Subject calibration: the FIFA pipeline's one-second body scan is the productionised version of a long-standing finding — knowing each athlete's segment lengths a-priori roughly halves joint-centre error.
  • Throughput: a markerless lab session captures 5–10× as many movement reps per hour as a marker lab, because there are no marker placements to redo.

Validation

The validation literature has converged. Theia3D, KinaTrax, OpenCap and OpenPose-based pipelines now consistently meet sub-5° RMSE versus marker-based Vicon/Qualisys for sagittal-plane lower-limb kinematics in walking, running and CMJ. Frontal- and transverse-plane angles, hip/spine angles, and any movement involving heavy soft-tissue artefact remain more variable. Recent open-access work (Tandfonline 2026 on tennis-stroke mechanical work, PLOS One on skeleton push-start) extends the validation envelope into sport-specific contexts and confirms the field-vs-lab agreement is now acceptable for monitoring trends and within-athlete change — but not yet for diagnosing absolute kinematic abnormality.

What's new in 2026

Three shifts. First, the productionisation push: Theia3D's integration with bat-and-ball tracking (April), Lenovo's per-player avatar pipeline at the World Cup (June), and the Hawk-Eye optical mesh now serving as a skeletal-pose source for MLB ABS as well as football SAOT mean MMC is no longer a research artefact — it is operational stadium infrastructure. Second, subject-specific scaling has gone from a manual lab calibration to a 1-second pre-tournament body scan, which removes the largest historical error source. Third, the open-source stack (OpenCap, MMPose, University of Bath's "biomechanics in the wild" tooling) has reached the point where a well-resourced academy can build its own usable pipeline with two action cameras and an afternoon's GPU time. The validation envelope for these open tools is narrower than for Theia3D, but the cost difference is real.

The practitioner takeaway: MMC is now the right default for any new movement-analysis investment in a high-performance environment. Marker-based labs remain the reference for joint-load estimation and for any project that ends in publication; MMC is for the 95 % of your applied work where you need throughput, repeatability and an athlete who isn't covered in retroreflective dots. The remaining live question is data ownership: when your provider hands you joint angles derived from their black-box pose estimator, you are no longer in control of the measurement chain. Insist on validation reports, fixed model versions and exportable raw video.
