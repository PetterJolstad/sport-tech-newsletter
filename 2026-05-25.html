The big stories

Catapult posts record FY26 — the consolidation bet starts paying off

Reported 20 May, Catapult's full-year results (year to 31 March 2026) showed record revenue of US$140.7m (+19% constant currency), management EBITDA up 67% to US$24.7m as margin expanded from 13% to 18%, and annualised contract value up 28% to US$133.8m (18% organic, the rest from the Perch and IMPECT acquisitions). It added 576 new pro teams (now >5,500), holds retention above 96%, and sits on >US$53m cash with no debt. The read for practitioners isn't the numbers — it's the strategy they fund: GPS load, weight-room velocity (Perch) and tactical/event data (IMPECT) are being folded into one backend. Expect tighter cross-domain workflows, and less leverage to mix best-of-breed point solutions as the market keeps consolidating. Shares rallied on the result, though they remain down roughly a third over the past year — the market is rewarding the turn to profit, not the growth story alone.

COROS ships an official MCP — your training data is now queryable by ChatGPT and Claude

Announced 19 May, COROS became the first major wearable brand to ship an officially supported Model Context Protocol server. It exposes ~15 read-only endpoints across five categories — profile and paired hardware, activities, daily health metrics, EvoLab assessments and your current training plan — to ChatGPT Plus and Claude Pro for users in North America and Europe, with AI write-back of plans on the roadmap. For practitioners, this is the first sanctioned path for an LLM to query an athlete's raw device data through their own credentials — no exports, no scripting. That's genuinely useful for athlete self-service, but it quietly raises three questions you now own: athlete consent, where the data flows, and the validity of AI-narrated "readiness" and training advice built on a single device's numbers. If athletes start arriving with chatbot-generated training opinions, this is where they came from.

Strava overhauls strength training — 14 integrations, including WHOOP, Garmin and COROS

On 21 May Strava rebuilt its strength experience: a dedicated workout log (sets, reps, weight), auto-generated muscle maps that highlight trained muscle groups, five new shareables, and 14 launch integrations spanning wearables (WHOOP, Garmin, Amazfit), strength apps (HEVY, Fitbod, Motra) and an in-gym tie-in with 24 Hour Fitness. Strength was already one of Strava's fastest-growing activity types (500m+ uploads in 2025). The practitioner angle: Strava is becoming a strength aggregation layer athletes already live in, so resistance-training data will increasingly surface alongside endurance. But keep the5krunner's caveat front of mind — this is self-reported logging, not instrumented load. There's no bar velocity and no objective intensity, so treat the muscle map as an adherence and communication tool, not a training-load metric.

The Enhanced Games break a swimming world record in Las Vegas

At the inaugural Enhanced Games (21–24 May, Las Vegas) — where athletes openly use performance-enhancing drugs under medical supervision — Greek sprinter Kristian Gkolomeev swam the 50m freestyle in 20.81s on 24 May, inside Cameron McEvoy's 20.88s mark, banking US$250,000 for the win plus a US$1m world-record bonus; sprinter Fred Kerley fell short on the track. Set aside the ethics for a moment: the Games are now a live, public data point on the magnitude of supervised pharmacological gains, and a reminder that "physiologically possible" and "clean" are visibly diverging. It pairs uncomfortably well with this week's Sports Medicine paper on undetectable angiogenic doping (below). Expect athletes, parents and media to ask you what it means — worth having a considered answer ready.

Obstructive Sleep Apnea: A Potential Sleeping Giant of Sport Performance and Health?

Driller M, Pearce AJ, Lovato N · Sports Medicine · Online 19 May 2026

A short communication arguing that OSA is an under-recognised driver of poor recovery, daytime fatigue and blunted adaptation in athletes — and that prospective work in female, endurance and Paralympic athletes is overdue. The practical read: if your wearable sleep-staging and HRV data keep flagging an athlete as flat despite sensible load, consider screening for OSA rather than attributing it to training. Sleep architecture, not just sleep duration, belongs in the readiness conversation.

Tech-talk · sensor deep-dive

Smart rings: the finger-worn readiness sensor Oura just took to the IPO desk

With Oura filing to go public this week and ring data now flowing into LLMs via COROS-style integrations, the finger-worn recovery ring is worth a proper look. It's the rare consumer form factor that has earned a place on elite performance desks — but the score on the app is doing a lot of quiet work you should understand.

What it is

A smart ring packs a photoplethysmography (PPG) stack — green/red/infrared LEDs and photodiodes — against the palmar side of the finger, where the digital arteries sit close to the surface and vascular density is high. Add a negative-temperature-coefficient skin-temperature sensor and a 3-axis accelerometer, and you have a device optimised for capturing overnight resting physiology: nocturnal resting heart rate, heart-rate variability (rMSSD), respiratory rate, skin-temperature deviation and a sleep estimate, distilled into a proprietary "readiness" or "recovery" score. Crucially, finger PPG has a better signal-to-noise ratio at rest than wrist PPG, which is why rings often out-measure watches for nocturnal HR and HRV.

Who's in the market

Oura (Gen 4) is the validated incumbent, with the deepest research footprint and a team/enterprise arm via the former Sparta Science platform (Trinsic). Ultrahuman (Ring AIR / Ring Pro) competes on no-subscription pricing and more open data, with a CGM tie-in. Samsung Galaxy Ring plays the ecosystem card; RingConn and Circular round out the field on price and features.

Specs that matter to practitioners

  • It's a rest sensor, not an exercise sensor. Finger PPG degrades badly with motion and cold-finger vasoconstriction — rings are for sleep and overnight baselines, not session intensity.
  • Temperature is relative. Skin-temp is reported as a deviation from baseline (useful for illness onset and menstrual-cycle phase), not an absolute core reading.
  • Data access differs. Oura's API supports team dashboards; Ultrahuman is more open. Check whether you can pull raw nightly HRV, or only the score.
  • Subscriptions and firmware. Most of the value sits behind a subscription, and algorithm updates can shift an athlete's baseline overnight — log firmware versions if you're trending across a season.

Validation & reasonable skepticism

Oura's nocturnal resting HR and rMSSD show strong agreement with ECG at rest, and it tends to top independent head-to-heads for sleep-period HRV. Sleep staging is weaker — roughly 65–76% four-stage agreement versus polysomnography — and frequency-domain HRV (LF, LF:HF) is unreliable. Ultrahuman tracks Oura closely on HRV/RHR but lacks equivalent peer-reviewed sleep-staging validation. The headline caveat: the readiness score is an opaque composite, not an independently validated metric. Treat the underlying nightly RHR and rMSSD as the defensible signal; treat the single number as a conversation-starter, not ground truth — and if you need court-defensible daily readiness, a chest-strap RR-interval measure remains the criterion.
