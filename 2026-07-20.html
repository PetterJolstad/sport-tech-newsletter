Suunto ships Q2 2026 firmware (v2.56.18) to Race 2 & Vertical 2 — 200 routes, contour maps, Sony GNSS chipset firmware

Staged rollout began 14 July 2026. On-watch route storage jumps from 15 to 200, offline maps gain contour shading and toggleable street/trail/river layers, a new Tide widget lands (relevant to coastal S&C, ocean-based endurance camps and open-water triathlon planning), and — the practitioner headline — Sony's updated GNSS chipset firmware ships in the same package, which Suunto claims cuts satellite acquisition time and improves track fidelity in dense-canopy and urban-canyon environments. NBA Summer League doubles as a 35-vendor tech expo — Launchpad Demo Day and a sensor-embedded basketball on live courts

Reported out of Las Vegas on 13 July: 35 companies exhibited coaching, protective, biomechanics and tracking products alongside the Summer League slate that ran through 19 July, capped by Launchpad Cohort 5's Demo Day. Cohort 5 headliners include Peripheral Labs (Toronto — spatial-intelligence platform borrowing autonomous-vehicle stack to reconstruct games into photorealistic 3D with player and ball tracking), Swish Basket (Tel Aviv — camera + LiDAR shot analytics on a consumer app), and — the one to watch — Atlas (San Francisco — dry-electrode EEG in a behind-the-ear form factor outputting a "Clarity" cognitive-readiness score).

Also live on the Vegas floor: the NBA quietly rolled a connected basketball with an embedded contact-sensing IMU across all Summer League games. The league is transparent about the near-term application (last-touch out-of-bounds decisions) and equally transparent that the ball feel/weight is unchanged. This is the NBA's version of the FIFA Connected Ball path — same 14 g class of embedded sensor, same officiating-first justification, same governing-body ownership of the raw stream.

🌱 Up-and-coming — Atlas

Atlas: prototype-to-early-pilot, six-month NBA/WNBA collaboration under way. Genuine novelty is the form factor — most sports-grade EEG lives in caps or hairbands that require gel or dense electrodes; a behind-the-ear dry-electrode headset that captures useful frontal-lobe signal in the field would be a genuine step. Reasonable skepticism: (a) the composite "Clarity" score is proprietary and unpublished, so it's a black box vs. the underlying EEG channels a lab would want; (b) motion-artefact rejection during high-intensity contact is the historical failure mode of consumer EEG and there's no independent validation yet; (c) the pilot cohort model at NBA Launchpad has previously produced both winners (Sports Innovation Lab, Verb) and silent exits — a demo day is not a deployment.

Forme Science becomes WNBPA's official posture & recovery partner — pilot activates at All-Star 25 July

Announced mid-week (~15 July, per Athletech News). Forme Science — best known for compression garments with embedded "posture intelligence" driven by a passive band that biases scapular retraction and thoracic extension — signed a multi-year deal with the Women's National Basketball Players Association. A player-ambassador activation runs at WNBA All-Star Weekend on 25 July in Chicago, with a full roster rollout targeted for 2027. Three explicitly-scoped use cases: travel-related spinal decompression, sleep-related postural stress reducing overnight HRV, and in-season durability.

The signal is not the garments per se — it's that a players' union is now sourcing recovery infrastructure directly. Practitioners running WNBA cross-collaborations (medical, S&C consulting) should expect Forme dashboards to appear in return-to-play data feeds this season. As always with posture wearables: the strongest evidence base is subjective comfort and adherence; downstream performance claims remain thin. Treat it as adjunct kit, not a training-load surrogate.

An accelerometer-equipped running watch provides a valid estimate of peak vertical ground reaction force and cumulative weighted damage: a cross-sectional laboratory study

Willy RW, Moore IS, Leske J, Polson T, Ruby BC.

European Journal of Sport Science — 2026, 26: e70215.

Eighteen well-trained runners on an instrumented treadmill at 2.7–4.0 m/s plus training pace. Tech-talk: Muscle-oxygenation wearables (NIRS)

Moxy · Train.Red (Artinis) · NNOXX · Humon (assets → WHOOP) · greenTEG spin-outs · the WHOOP + Garmin race

Continuous-wave near-infrared spectroscopy has been physiologists' compact way of watching what's happening inside the working muscle for two decades. In the last year the field has shifted from "endurance-lab niche kit" to "the next signal both major recovery-band vendors want in their consumer stack." That's the reason it's this week's topic — not a single new launch, but a category that has quietly become the next competitive frontier in wearables while a small handful of shipping products fight for the applied-practice foothold.

What it is

Two narrow-band LEDs — typically ~660 nm and ~855 nm — sit on the skin over a target muscle belly. Oxygenated and deoxygenated haemoglobin absorb the two wavelengths differently, so a linear array of photodetectors positioned 5–50 mm from the emitter can back out muscle oxygen saturation (SmO2, %) and, in some devices, total-haemoglobin index (tHb). The result is a continuous, non-invasive readout of local O2 supply vs demand at the working tissue — the physiological gap HRV, SpO2 and RPE cannot fill because they read whole-body or vascular signals.

Providers today

  • Moxy Monitor (Fortiori Design, Minnesota) — the reference wearable NIRS since 2013. 15 mm × 60 mm probe, ANT+ / BLE, agnostic to head-unit. The de-facto research-friendly device.
  • Train.Red (FYER / PLUS models, Netherlands) — sensor hardware from Artinis Medical Systems, the same lab that makes the PORTAMON. Adds an app-side coaching layer and cheaper price point ($299–599).
  • NNOXX One (Rhode Island) — the only shipping consumer wearable that also claims a nitric-oxide channel alongside SmO2, with an AI-coach app built on top. Peer-reviewed SmO2 validation; NO claim less validated.
  • WHOOP — no shipping product. US patent 12,594,037 B2 granted 7 April 2026 to Whoop, listing Humon's founding team as inventors after Whoop absorbed Humon's assets in 2020. Patent explicitly describes thigh/chest/arm placement, not the wrist.
  • Garmin — no shipping product. "Muscle Battery" trademark filed early 2026; multiple analyst read-throughs (the5krunner, gadgetsandwearables) suggest a strap-mounted SmO2 accessory is on the roadmap for late 2026 or 2027.

Specs to insist on

Continuous-wave NIRS is inexpensive but path-length-normalisation-limited; frequency-domain and time-domain NIRS are more accurate but rarely field-ready. Insist on: two-wavelength (not one), a minimum inter-optode separation of 25 mm for adult vastus lateralis (deeper penetration), skinfold thickness measured before every session (the biggest single confound), and a device that reports raw SmO2 % rather than a smoothed proprietary score. Sampling at ≥1 Hz is enough for training; 10 Hz for interval reconstruction.

Validation status

Muscle Oximetry in Sports Science (Perrey & Ferrari, updated systematic review, Sports Medicine) rates NIRS as a validated non-invasive measure of skeletal-muscle O2 supply/utilisation dynamics — the SmO2 desaturation slope during incremental exercise coincides with lactate-based thresholds in trained cyclists (Feldmann et al., EJAP 2023). Where the evidence weakens: static values, inter-day repeatability of absolute SmO2, and interpretation across muscle sites (locomotor is more reliable than accessory sites — Frontiers in Physiology 2024). Skinfold thickness at the sensor site introduces 5–15 % error unmodelled. A November 2025 Bioengineering paper cross-validated a wearable CW-NIRS sensor against gold-standard frequency-domain NIRS in cyclists and found strong agreement across the incremental protocol but growing bias at high intensity.

What's new

Three vectors converging: (1) the WHOOP patent + the Humon-into-WHOOP acqui-hire signal that a consumer wearable-format NIRS is being seriously engineered by the strain-and-recovery leader; (2) Garmin's Muscle Battery trademark and the analyst consensus that Garmin will need its own SmO2 module to keep Physio pace; (3) NNOXX's positioning as the only currently-shipping consumer NIRS device with a serious app layer while both giants remain paperware. Add the Muscle Oxygen Monitors category-market analysis pegging 14.8 % CAGR through 2035 and the direction is clear: NIRS is moving from "niche endurance-lab kit for the physiologist" toward "expected feature in the next-generation recovery wristband." Practitioner takeaway: incorporate a Moxy or Train.Red probe into your threshold and interval-quality workflows now if you haven't; treat any 2026 consumer-wrist NIRS claim skeptically until independent validation on the specific device drops.
