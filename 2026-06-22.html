Amazfit's Helio Strap Pro lands a dual-sensor body architecture in a $199, subscription-free package

Amazfit unveiled the Helio Strap Pro at the PUMA HYROX World Championships in Stockholm on 18 June 2026, with US pre-orders opening immediately at $199.99 and shipping from 25 June. Unlike its predecessor, the Pro is not a single strap — it is a two-piece system: the Helio Core Motion HR (a 5 Hz arm-worn PPG strap) plus the Helio Core Motion Waist, a screen-free 9-axis IMU worn at the lumbar spine that runs ~40 days on a charge and can record entirely offline. The waist unit is what's new: it segments and scores each of the eight HYROX stations (SkiErg, sled push/pull, burpee broad jumps, row, farmer's carry, sandbag lunges, wall balls) and feeds movement-quality metrics back into the Zepp app via the Balance 3 / Balance Ultra watches. Amazfit is in year two of its multi-year HYROX timekeeping deal.

For practitioners: ignore the HYROX-specific scoring for a moment. The interesting bit is the architecture. A trunk-mounted IMU paired with an upper-arm HR strap, both BLE, both consumer-priced, both with no subscription, is the same fundamental sensor topology your S&C floor has been asking GymAware / Output Sports / Catapult Vector about for years — just unbundled and aimed at hybrid-fitness consumers. The waist sensor's IMU stream is the real test bed; if Zepp ever opens raw access (currently the data is HYROX-segmented and Zepp-app-locked), this becomes the cheapest credible trunk-IMU on the market for jump landings, sled-acceleration profiling and lifting-bar proxy work. Worth a punt for a single rack or a hybrid-athlete cohort. Don't replace your validated kit with it yet.

Stanford-founded Clair Health raises $11.6 M to put a continuous, non-invasive hormone monitor on the wrist

Clair Health, founded by Stanford grads Jenny Duan (21) and Abhinav Agarwal, announced an $11.6 M seed round on 17 June 2026 led by Khosla Ventures, with participation from a16z speedrun, Reach Capital, Brydge Club, Treehub, Cartan Capital, AGI House, Insiders VC, and angels including 23andMe founder Anne Wojcicki. The product is a jewellery-styled wrist wearable with ten on-board biosensors — including a biomagnetic channel Clair claims is not present in any consumer competitor — that streams skin temperature, HR / HRV, electrodermal activity, sleep and respiratory features into ML models trained to infer estrogen, progesterone, LH, FSH and PdG levels across the cycle. Crucially: Clair is not measuring the hormones directly. It is inferring them. Beta units are out, commercial ship is November 2026 at $369 + $9.99/month, the waitlist is past 25,000, and a Stanford Medicine clinical trial is planned alongside an FDA submission to follow the wellness launch.

Maturity: closed beta; commercial ship November 2026. Who's piloting: beta cohort plus a planned Stanford Medicine trial — no public elite-sport partnership yet, though the company is explicitly pitching the women's-performance use case. Genuine novelty: hormone-aware training has been a paper-and-questionnaire exercise. Direct continuous measurement (e.g. the Caltech sweat-aptamer estradiol patch) is still pre-commercial. Clair's bet is that the ML-inferred path — running existing CGM-grade sensors through a hormone-state model — gets practitioners a usable cycle-phase signal a decade before direct-measurement biosensors clear regulatory. Reasonable skepticism: the entire output is a model prediction, not a measurement. Validation will live or die on how well the inferred curves track gold-standard salivary or serum assays in unblinded studies — none of which have published yet. Until those land, treat any "Clair says you're in your luteal phase" tag the same way you treat a wrist HRV reading: directionally useful, not a number to chase. The category is real, the company is credible, the data isn't there yet.

19 June 2026 · Officiating & tracking · FIFA World Cup 26

One week in: the Trionda ball quietly saved an offside, the ref-cam delivered the footage, and the integrated stack started looking like infrastructure rather than spectacle

Eight days into the 16-stadium World Cup, the first operational reviews of the FIFA tech stack are landing. The most consequential incident: Sweden v Tunisia in Monterrey on 15 June, where Sweden's Mattias Svanberg scored 12 seconds after coming on, was flagged offside by a linesman who could not have seen the timing of Isak's first touch — and the Trionda's 500 Hz IMU then provided the exact ball-contact timestamp that redrew the SAOT offside line and confirmed Svanberg was onside. SAOT did what last week's brief said it would do: it disagreed with the human read, in the direction of the player. Separately, Lenovo's referee body-cam, stabilised in real time on the IBC's private 5G uplink, produced a viable broadcast feed of South Korea's Hwang In-beom goal against Czechia from the centre referee's first-person point of view — the first time a major-tournament broadcast has cut to that angle live.

For practitioners: the SAOT-vs-broadcast precision gap predicted in last week's edition is now showing up in match reports. If your video-analysis pipeline is built on broadcast-tagged camera angles, you have a week or two before clips start arriving in your inbox where the SAOT call on the pitch contradicts the freeze-frame in your tagging software. That gap is not a bug in the SAOT; it is a permanent change in the precision floor of an offside event, and your tagging conventions need to follow. The ref-cam footage is also producing the first broadcast-grade first-person video of elite refereeing decisions — that is a teaching dataset, not just a highlight reel, and it will end up in coaching-education modules within the year.

RunSafePro brings rear-radar, GPS panic-alerts and incident detection to a $119 running vest

Netherlands-based RunSafePro launched on Kickstarter on 16 June 2026 with a four-feature smart vest: a 30 m rear radar lifted from the cycling radar category (Garmin Varia-style), a GPS panic button that pings up to 50 pre-set contacts, automatic incident detection that triggers if motion stops without a check-in, and an adaptive 360° backlight that brightens with ambient darkness. Genuine novelty: radar-equipped runner safety vests aren't conceptually new (the cycling category has had them since Varia in 2015), but RunSafePro is the first to bundle radar, incident detection, GPS-SOS and adaptive visibility into a single sub-$200 wearable explicitly framed as a women's-safety product. For practitioners working with female endurance athletes who routinely train on roads at dawn or dusk, it's worth tracking; the safety question is increasingly upstream of performance.

Tech-talk: continuous hormone-aware wearables — direct measurement vs ML inference

Pegged to Clair Health's $11.6 M Khosla-led seed round announced 17 June 2026.

What it is

"Hormone-aware training" has been a software story so far. The wearables menstrual-cycle tags shipping today on Oura, WHOOP, Apple Watch and Garmin all rely on user-logged period-start dates and a textbook 28-day model, which they then drape over a recovery dashboard. None of them actually measures a hormone. The new category that emerged this week — Clair Health's $11.6 M raise being the cleanest pin in the calendar — is the first credible attempt to put a continuous hormone signal on the wrist. It splits cleanly into two technical approaches: direct biosensor measurement, and machine-learning inference from physiology.

The two approaches

Direct biosensor measurement reads the hormone (or a metabolite) in a body-fluid compartment using sensor chemistry. The leading published work is the Caltech / Wei Gao lab's wearable sweat patch using inkjet-printed gold nanoparticles, MXene titanium-carbide films and aptamer-based recognition elements to track estradiol in eccrine sweat — validated across the full reproductive cycle in lab testing, with sweat-estradiol changing roughly ten-fold from menstruation to ovulation. ProgenCare (2025) is the equivalent for salivary progesterone, using Ti3C2-MXene to amplify a signal that sits at 1–2 % of plasma concentration. Both are credible research demonstrations; neither has cleared a regulator and neither is in commercial pilots.

ML inference measures the hormone indirectly, by running standard wearable physiology — skin temperature, HR / HRV, electrodermal activity, sleep architecture, respiratory rate — through a model trained to predict cycle phase and hormone state. This is the Clair Health architecture. Ten on-board biosensors (including a biomagnetic channel Clair flags as proprietary), 130-plus engineered biomarkers, a model trained to output estrogen, progesterone, LH, FSH and PdG curves and a stated nine sub-phases of the cycle — more than double the four typically taught.

Who's actually shipping

  • Clair Health (US, Stanford-founded) — wristband, $369 + $9.99/month, ships Nov 2026, ~25,000-deep waitlist. Wellness launch first, FDA submission to follow.
  • Caltech (Gao lab) — sweat aptamer patch for estradiol; research prototype, no commercial entity yet.
  • ProgenCare (academic, CN) — microfluidic electrochemical salivary progesterone sensor; lab-validated, no consumer product.
  • Oura / WHOOP / Garmin / Apple — user-logged cycle apps with light skin-temperature anchoring; not hormone monitors in any meaningful sense.

Spec snapshot

  • Compartments: sweat (estradiol patches), saliva (progesterone), interstitial fluid (theoretical for hormones; nothing shipping). ML inference uses no body-fluid sampling at all.
  • Output cadence: direct sensors give a per-sweat-event or per-sample reading; ML inference produces a continuous curve, but the curve is a prediction smoothed across hours-to-days.
  • Sensitivity floor: estradiol moves over ~10× across the cycle (~20–400 pg/mL plasma equivalent), progesterone over ~30× — both well above what aptamer chemistry can resolve in the lab, well below what is reliably extractable from skin-temperature alone without a learned model and a large training corpus.
  • Power: Clair's wristband sits in the same battery / form factor envelope as a smartwatch; direct sweat patches in published work run a day or two on a coin cell.

Validation picture

This is the honest part. Direct sensors have peer-reviewed in-lab data tying patch output to standard ELISA — that's good — but no peer-reviewed published cohort of more than a handful of free-living athletes wearing the patch across multiple cycles. ML inference (Clair's path) has no published validation paper yet. The closest analogy is the early CGM era: the underlying skin-temperature signal does shift across the cycle and the autonomic signal does too — that part is well-established — but inferring a four- or nine-phase hormonal state from those second-order signals is exactly the kind of problem where calibration cohort, ethnicity coverage and contraception status all matter enormously. The Stanford Medicine trial Clair has announced is the right thing to watch; until it reports, treat the device output as directional, not quantitative.

What's new this week

Clair's $11.6 M Khosla-led raise (with Reach Capital, a16z speedrun and Anne Wojcicki on the cap table) is the first venture round in this category at this size, and the first to land with a clear commercial-ship date (Nov 2026) and a published clinical-trial pathway. For practitioners working with female-athlete cohorts, that's the inflection: hormone-aware programming moves from a software-on-questionnaire problem to a hardware-with-validation-roadmap problem inside the next 12 months. Case-study tier in late 2026 — squad-wide deployment still a 2027–2028 conversation, dependent on what the Stanford trial actually finds.
