The big stories

Garmin pushes the Human Performance Lab stack down to $250 with Forerunner 70 & 170

Announced 12 May and on sale 15 May, the entry-level Forerunner 70 ($249.99) and 170 ($299.99 / $349.99 Music) now include training readiness, training status, HRV status, wrist-based running power and full running dynamics — features that until this week required a Forerunner 265 or pricier. For practitioners managing youth, academy or amateur cohorts on a budget, this collapses the price floor for credible internal-load monitoring, and wrist-based running power finally puts a Stryd-style metric on a sub-$300 watch — with the usual caveats about wrist-PPG quality during intervals and the algorithmic estimation of mechanical power.

Google's $99 Fitbit Air puts the WHOOP subscription model on the defensive

The screenless, no-subscription Fitbit Air launched 7 May and starts shipping 26 May with continuous HR, HRV, SpO2, sleep staging and passive optical AFib detection — bundled with three months of Google Health Premium and Pixel Watch pairing. By 13–14 May, DC Rainmaker had documented WHOOP's hasty Reddit/X reply and a CEO video promising future features. The practical read for performance staff: WHOOP isn't displaced for elite cohorts (26 Hz PPG sampling, on-demand ECG on MG, coaching ecosystem still matter), but for amateur and college rosters the ROI calculus on $359/year just got harder. Expect more athletes arriving with Fitbit Air data and asking you to integrate it.

Garmin's screen-less WHOOP rival "Cirqa" leaks ahead of summer launch

Leaked product imagery and FCC filings published this week (covered by Android Central, Tom's Guide and Garmin Rumors between 11–14 May) point to a fabric-banded, displayless Garmin tracker positioned directly against WHOOP and the Fitbit Air. Pricing chatter sits around five times the Fitbit Air — suggesting Garmin is targeting the higher-margin WHOOP subscriber rather than the mass market. For practitioners, the more interesting question isn't the hardware: it's whether Garmin Connect's training-readiness and HRV-status frameworks become viable on a screenless form factor, which would meaningfully lower the friction of getting non-watch-wearing athletes onto a single backend.

Hudl locks up Ghana & Senegal U18 scouting through Young African Promises deal

Announced 11 May, Hudl secured exclusive multi-year rights to all video and data from Young African Promises' Future Talent Tournaments (two U18 events per year in Accra), with footage flowing directly into Hudl Wyscout. This is the latest move in Hudl's vertical lock-up of the scouting funnel after the StatsBomb integration and the formal Wyscout rebrand earlier in the month. Recruiters at academies and pro clubs not on Wyscout will increasingly find themselves looking at West African talent through Hudl's window only. Tech-talk · sensor deep-dive

Instrumented mouthguards (iMGs) for head impact monitoring

With the women's Rugby World Cup approaching in August and World Rugby's iMG mandate now embedded in elite-game protocols, the instrumented mouthguard is the sensor most likely to land on your medical or S&C desk in the next 12 months — even if you don't work in rugby. Football (soccer), Australian rules, ice hockey, lacrosse and US college football programs are all piloting them. Here's the practitioner's short guide.

What it is

An iMG is a custom or boil-and-bite mouthguard with embedded tri-axial accelerometers (typically up to 200 g, sampling ~3,200 Hz) and a tri-axial gyroscope (up to ~35,000 °/s). Because it couples directly to the upper jaw — which is rigidly attached to the skull — it gives a far better estimate of true head-kinematic exposure than a skin-, helmet- or skull-cap-mounted sensor. Each suspected impact is logged with peak linear acceleration, peak rotational velocity/acceleration, impact location, duration, and a video-verified classification flag.

Who's in the market

Prevent Biometrics (US) — the World Rugby reference device and the only iMG that meets the full World Rugby Instrumented Mouthguard Performance Specification. Worn across the men's and women's international game, the Six Nations, and most Tier-1 clubs.

HitIQ Nexus A9 (Australia) — competing platform with deep deployment in the AFL and Australian rugby. Strong dashboard, ASX-listed, more aggressive on commercial pricing for non-pro programs (~A$599 entry).

ORB Innovations (UK) — newer entrant, lighter footprint, gaining traction in football and women's contact-sport pilots.

Stanford Football iMG & Diversified Technical Systems (DTS) — research-grade systems used in NCAA biomechanics labs rather than team operations.

Specs that matter to practitioners

  • Sampling rate: 3,200 Hz on the accelerometer is the de-facto floor. Below ~1,000 Hz you cannot reliably reconstruct the sub-10 ms impact peak.
  • Coupling: a poorly fitted mouthguard generates false positives (chewing, shouting, tongue strikes). Video verification is non-negotiable — never report iMG events without it.
  • Threshold: most platforms default to a 10 g event trigger. Below this the noise floor dominates; above ~70 g the clinical question shifts from monitoring to immediate HIA escalation.
  • Battery and download: typical match-day capacity is one full game (~80 min rugby). Most platforms now offer in-mouth Bluetooth streaming to a pitchside tablet for live HIA support.

What's new

World Rugby's protocol now mandates that any in-match event exceeding the league's threshold automatically triggers an off-field HIA, regardless of medic-side suspicion. That has converted iMGs from a research tool into a piece of officiating infrastructure — and quietly raised the bar for sensor reliability, latency and chain-of-custody on the data. Expect equivalent moves from World Lacrosse and several US college conferences over the next 18 months.

Reasonable skepticism

iMG single-impact peak kinematics still correlate poorly with concussion outcome at the individual level — cumulative-exposure metrics over a season look more promising but remain pre-validation. The vendor dashboards present a tidy "exposure" number that can encourage spurious comparisons between athletes with very different baseline brain reserve. Treat iMGs as an objective exposure log to be paired with symptom and neurocognitive data, not as a concussion diagnostic.
