Week ending Monday 18 May 2026 · Edition #1

Sport Tech Weekly

A quiet week for genuine innovation — but Google's $99 punch at WHOOP, a Garmin entry-level watch with the full Human Performance Lab stack, and Hudl's African scouting land-grab are reshaping the consumer and pro tiers in parallel.

The big stories

Garmin pushes the Human Performance Lab stack down to $250 with Forerunner 70 & 170

Announced 12 May and on sale 15 May, the entry-level Forerunner 70 ($249.99) and 170 ($299.99 / $349.99 Music) now include training readiness, training status, HRV status, wrist-based running power and full running dynamics — features that until this week required a Forerunner 265 or pricier. For practitioners managing youth, academy or amateur cohorts on a budget, this collapses the price-floor for credible internal-load monitoring; wrist-based running power finally puts a Stryd-style metric on a sub-$300 watch (with the usual caveats about wrist-PPG quality during intervals).

Sources: Garmin newsroom · the5krunner deep dive · DC Rainmaker: what changed

Google's $99 Fitbit Air puts the WHOOP subscription model on the defensive

The screenless, no-subscription Fitbit Air launched 7 May and started shipping mid-week with continuous HR, HRV, SpO2, sleep staging and passive optical AFib detection — bundled with three months of Google Health Premium. By 13–14 May, DC Rainmaker had documented WHOOP's hasty Reddit/X response and a CEO video promising future features. The practical read for performance staff: WHOOP isn't displaced for elite cohorts (26 Hz PPG sampling, on-demand ECG on MG, the coaching ecosystem still matter), but for amateur/college rosters the ROI calculus on $359/year just got harder. Expect more athletes arriving with Fitbit Air data and asking you to integrate it.

Sources: TechCrunch launch coverage · DC Rainmaker: WHOOP on the defensive · Fitbit Air: everything you need to know

Hudl locks up Ghana & Senegal U18 scouting through Young African Promises deal

Announced 11 May, Hudl secured exclusive multi-year rights to all video and data from Young African Promises' Future Talent Tournaments (two U18 events per year in Accra), with footage flowing into Hudl Wyscout. This continues Hudl's vertical lock-up of the scouting funnel after the StatsBomb integration — recruiters at academies and pro clubs that aren't on Wyscout will increasingly find themselves looking at West African talent through Hudl's window only. Worth raising with your recruitment department before the next budget cycle.

Sources: Hudl announcement

NBA Draft Combine (10–17 May) ran its first season on AWS-backed live data hub

This year's combine in Chicago piped wingspan, vertical, agility and strength testing into a public AWS Data Hub in close to real time, with team-side computer-vision tools (some teams openly testing ChatGPT and Claude pipelines) pulling measurable stats from scouting footage. For practitioners outside basketball: the combine remains the cleanest annual benchmark for elite young athlete anthropometrics and explosive testing — and the data is now downloadable rather than locked in NBA dot-com tables.

Sources: NBA Combine Data Hub · Sportico on team-side AI use

Tech-talk: Core body temperature sensors

With Northern-hemisphere summer competition and heat-acclimation blocks ramping up, the heat-flux estimate of core body temperature has gone from niche endurance toy to a standard tool in most well-equipped performance departments. Here's the practitioner's short guide.

What it is

Wearable "core" body-temperature monitors don't actually measure the core. They sample skin temperature plus heat flux (the rate of thermal energy leaving the body through a chest- or arm-worn pad), feed those signals along with heart rate into a proprietary algorithm, and output an estimate of rectal/gastrointestinal core temperature. The algorithm is the product — the hardware is a fairly simple combined thermistor and heat-flux plate.

Who's in the market

greenteg CORE 2 (Switzerland) is the consumer/team standard. Released March 2025, the second-generation device added a redesigned clip, LED status indicator and is the platform behind the new "Heat Adaptation Score" launched in late 2025. Pairs with Garmin, Wahoo, Apple Watch, Zwift, and most cycling head units via ANT+ / BLE. ~17 g, ~6 days battery, ~€280.

greenteg CALERA Research is the higher-resolution sibling — same physics, more raw data export, designed for lab use. The most-cited device in the academic literature.

Ingestible thermometric pills (e.g. e-Celsius Performance, BodyCap) remain the field-deployable gold standard against which the wearables are validated. Higher accuracy but single-use, ~€50/pill, and impractical for daily monitoring.

Specs that matter to practitioners

  • Bias vs. gold standard: independent studies typically report mean bias of 0.01–0.30 °C against rectal/gastrointestinal probes in cycling and running, widening at very high intensity, in cool conditions, or with poor sensor adhesion.
  • Warm-up time: the algorithm needs ~10–20 min to stabilise; pre-session readings are unreliable.
  • Sport context: validated mostly in steady-state cycling and running. Intermittent team-sport and combat-sport validation is the active research frontier — see this week's IJSPP beach volleyball paper.
  • Fitness effect: CALERA accuracy degrades in less-trained populations because algorithm calibration assumes cardiovascular response patterns of trained athletes (Verdel et al., 2025).

What's new this week

The IJSPP feasibility paper on CORE in simulated beach volleyball heat play is the headline literature item — first peer-reviewed data on CORE in a court-based intermittent sport, paired with a combined-cooling protocol assessed via CMJ and T-agility. Outside the strict 7-day window but worth re-reading alongside: greenteg's late-2025 Heat Adaptation Score, which turns multi-session CORE data into a single trended index.

Reasonable skepticism

The algorithm is a black box; you cannot independently audit it. Bias is small at steady-state but grows in conditions that matter most (sprints, hot+humid, post-cold-plunge rebound). Treat CORE as a relative trend tool for heat-acclimation dose monitoring and individual session pacing — not as a substitute for a pill when you need an absolute number for a return-to-play or medical decision.
