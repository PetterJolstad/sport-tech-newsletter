8–10 July 2026 · Wearables regulatory · Garmin

Garmin's Canadian and European Cirqa trademark filings reveal EDA emotional-stress sensing — a category no mainstream 24/7 wristband ships today

Two structural deadlines closed this week. Garmin filed its Canadian trademark application (CIPO 2483360) for the Cirqa band on 19 June, publicly indexed by CIPO in the middle of last week, and matched it with UK trademark UK00004405896 (23 June) and EUIPO 019384825 (23 June) — all three now visible in analyst filings pulled through 8–10 July 2026. The unusual line in the goods description is that "emotional stress" is enumerated separately from "physical stress." No other 24/7 wristband on the market — Whoop, Fitbit Air, Oura Ring, Ultrahuman R2 — lists a distinct emotional-stress sensor in its data specification, and Garmin's own current stack derives stress-score from HRV, which by construction cannot separate psychological from physical arousal.

The strongly-implied sensor is electrodermal activity (EDA), also known as galvanic skin response — a measure of skin conductance driven by the eccrine sweat glands on the palm and wrist that respond specifically to sympathetic nervous system activation. EDA has been the gold standard for arousal quantification in psychophysiology for six decades (Boucsein 2012 is the canonical reference), but the last mainstream 24/7 wristband to attempt it was the Fitbit Sense/Charge series, which required the user to manually press-and-hold a palm plate for a spot reading rather than a continuous background stream. Garmin's Cirqa filing implies a passive continuous EDA sensor on the wrist — a genuinely new category on a device that is meant to compete directly with Whoop and the newly-shipped Fitbit Air. Notebookcheck, gadgetsandwearables and TechTimes all independently corroborate the CIPO/EUIPO/UK trio; the5krunner published launch date modelling narrowing to 15 or 22 July 2026 based on the FCC confidentiality window and the UK trademark pre-publication timer.

For practitioners: this is the most consequential filing of Garmin's Cirqa saga so far. HRV is a beautiful integrative signal, but it is an integrative signal — it cannot separate "I ran hard this morning" from "my dashboard has 47 unopened emails" from "I am about to give a talk to 300 people." EDA, when it is done well, isolates the sympathetic arm of the autonomic nervous system on a much faster time-scale (phasic responses within 1–3 s, tonic drift over minutes) and specifically to non-thermal, non-metabolic stressors. Continuous wrist EDA has three known problems — motion artefact, sweat saturation and wearer variability — that Empatica's medical-grade EmbracePlus solves partially by chasing a research-grade skin-contact quality and a strap-tightness protocol most consumers will not follow. The question is whether Garmin has cracked the wrist-EDA validation problem or is going to ship a signal that reads well on marketing slides and rots in the field. If you support athletes running a training-load model on Garmin, tag Cirqa as a "watch and pilot" priority; the tech-talk below is the primer.

Sunday 13 July: Jannik Sinner defended the Wimbledon men's singles title over Alexander Zverev 6–7(7), 7–6(2), 6–3, 6–4 on a Centre Court running the fullest tech stack the tournament has ever deployed simultaneously. Linda Nosková beat Karolína Muchová for the women's singles title on Saturday. The operational novelties: (1) Hawk-Eye Live electronic line-calling projecting "out" and "fault" straight to the courtside scoreboards, with player challenges of line calls formally removed; (2) the new umpire-side Video Review on six courts (Centre, No 1, No 2, No 3, Court 12, Court 18) — unlimited player requests on double-bounce, foul-shot, not-up and touch calls, decisions from a courtside monitor in seconds; (3) the Player Analysis Technology trial permitting on-court wearables (Whoop most-visible, Abbott Lingo CGM patches spotted courtside). One notable in-match hiccup: a fourth-set line-call dispute went the chair umpire's way rather than through the video-review pipe, which reopens the "who reviews what" boundary conversation between ELC and the new video system.

For practitioners: two lessons carry forward. First, staged-rollout works — AELTC explicitly framed this year's video-review addition as a direct response to Year 1 of ELC feedback, and if you are advocating for tech rollout at your federation, this is the template to point at. Second, on the wearables trial: Sabalenka and Sinner both confirmed as Whoop wearers on tour; the operational shift the trial forces is a conversation with your athlete about which two or three biomarkers you actively coach during a changeover and which they must ignore. HRV is a lagging indicator; letting it drive an in-match tactical decision is almost always wrong.

7–8 July 2026 · Environmental physiology in-race · Tour de France 2026

Heat becomes a "logistical nightmare" at the Tour de France — Pogačar goes public, teams double down on forearm immersion, mist fans and team-specific ice vests as the peloton improvises its own protocol

Nine days into the Tour, defending champion Tadej Pogačar called cooling at this race a "logistical nightmare" in a 7 July Euronews interview, with forecasts of up to 41 °C around Carcassonne and active wildfires along the route. Every WorldTour team has ice vests on hand for pre-stage warm-ups now (Soudal Quick-Step and Netcompany-INEOS have both moved to team-specific PCM constructions with sub-zero gel that stays in gel form). The novel field-side technique of the second week: forearm immersion in iced water at the sign-in, aiming to dump heat through the AVA-rich glabrous skin of the palm and forearm without over-cooling the vastus muscle groups (Netcompany-INEOS have been photographed doing this; it is essentially palm-cooling physiology applied outside a CoolMitt). Jayco-AlUla went to cold moist towels at the neck; Team Visma runs a purpose-built high-pressure mist fan that injects cool water droplets into the airflow around each rider on the bus.

For practitioners: three points. First, the UCI's active High Temperature Protocol is now producing an observable behavioural response — teams are competing on cooling infrastructure the same way they compete on nutrition. Second, forearm immersion is a low-cost, high-benefit intervention any club, federation or academy heat programme can copy immediately: ice bath at 10–12 °C, forearms in for 3–5 min, right after warm-up. Third, on the ice-sock front: the UCI ban continues to be enforced on TTs and other "morphology-controlled" contexts, but riders were photographed with ice socks back in on the standard road stages. If you are writing a summer cooling SOP, the message is unchanged: mixed-method cooling (ice slurry pre-start plus PCM vest at the warm-up plus targeted glabrous-skin cooling in the last five minutes) still outperforms any single modality.

Tech-talk: electrodermal activity (EDA) — the sympathetic-arousal signal HRV cannot deliver, and whether a wristband can finally make it useful

Pegged to Garmin's Canadian and European Cirqa trademark filings surfacing an "emotional stress" sensor separate from physical stress, and to the mid-July Cirqa launch window narrowing to 15 or 22 July 2026.

What it is

Electrodermal activity — also called galvanic skin response (GSR) or skin conductance response (SCR) — is the change in electrical conductance of the skin driven by the eccrine sweat glands in the palms, fingertips, soles and wrist. Unlike heart rate variability, which is the composite output of both parasympathetic and sympathetic branches of the autonomic nervous system and cannot separate the two, EDA is essentially only sympathetic: eccrine sweat glands in the glabrous skin have no parasympathetic innervation. When the sympathetic nervous system fires — startle, cognitive load, emotional arousal, an approaching test, an angry email, the last twenty seconds before a start whistle — those glands secrete micro-quantities of ionic sweat and skin conductance rises. The signal decomposes into a slow-moving tonic component (skin conductance level, drifting over minutes) and a fast phasic component (skin conductance response, sub-second latency, 1–3 s rise and 3–5 s decay). Boucsein's Electrodermal Activity (2012) remains the reference textbook; the Society for Psychophysiological Research publication guidelines (Boucsein et al. 2012) set the field's methodological standards.

Why HRV isn't a substitute

The single most common practitioner misconception is that a "high stress score" from an HRV-based wearable already captures what EDA measures. It doesn't. HRV integrates both branches of the ANS; a suppressed HRV can mean "I did a heavy session this morning," "I slept 4 hours," "I have a low-grade infection" or "my sympathetic tone is elevated because I am about to walk on court" — the wearable cannot tell you which. EDA specifically tracks the sympathetic contribution and does so on a faster time-scale (1–3 s response latency vs the beat-to-beat window HRV needs to resolve), which is why it is the physiological readout of choice in lie-detection, VR emotion research, and the classic startle-response literature. For a performance practitioner, the useful question EDA answers is: is this athlete's autonomic elevation a physical-load residual or a psychological arousal signal? HRV cannot answer that. EDA, in principle, can.

Who's shipping (as of July 2026)

  • Empatica EmbracePlus (US/IT) — the medical-grade research standard: wrist-worn, continuous EDA at 4 Hz with a proper skin-contact protocol; primarily sold into clinical research and pharma. ~$3,000+ with a data-management subscription. The device to beat.
  • Fitbit Sense / Sense 2 / Charge 6 (US) — palm-plate spot-check EDA: user presses fingers on a metal plate for 30–120 s. Not continuous. Now largely superseded by the Fitbit Air stack.
  • BIOPAC MP-series + EDA100C (US) — the lab-standard tethered system for a sport-science research bench.
  • Shimmer3 GSR+ (IE) — the pragmatic middle ground: research-grade wrist unit, ~£400, popular in sport-psychology labs.
  • Movesense sensor + third-party EDA electrodes — modular and hackable; used by a handful of research groups but not a plug-and-play sport product.
  • Samsung Galaxy Watch (Ultra, 6, 7) — offers a stress score derived partly from EDA measured through the finger during a spot check, similar to Fitbit's approach.
  • Garmin Cirqa (US, expected 15 or 22 July 2026) — the trademark language strongly implies passive continuous wrist EDA in a 24/7 subscription-free wristband. If it delivers, it is the first mainstream continuous EDA on a $250–500 consumer device. Read as trademark-implied until Garmin publicly names the sensor.

Spec snapshot and known pitfalls

  • Signal: skin conductance measured in microsiemens (µS); tonic SCL typically 2–20 µS, phasic SCR amplitude 0.05–1.0 µS.
  • Sample rate: ≥ 4 Hz for phasic-response detection; ≥ 32 Hz for research-grade artefact rejection.
  • Site: palm and fingertip have the highest density of eccrine glands and give the strongest signal; wrist EDA is lower-amplitude and much more motion-vulnerable — which is the whole reason wrist-EDA on a 24/7 wearable is a hard engineering problem.
  • Motion artefact: the single biggest reason wrist-EDA has failed in consumer wearables to date. Empatica solves it partly with a proper strap-tightness protocol; any Garmin Cirqa validation will live or die here.
  • Sweat saturation: thermoregulatory sweat is not sympathetic sweat, and once sweat pooling starts (hot session, humid conditions) the EDA signal saturates and cannot distinguish arousal. This is the reason a running EDA trace during a hard training session is essentially uninterpretable.
  • Individual variability: resting SCL varies 5–10× between individuals; EDA must be interpreted intra-individually with a subject-specific baseline, never with a cross-athlete normative range.

Validation picture

The signal is one of the best-validated psychophysiological measures in existence — six decades of psychophysiology literature back the tonic-phasic decomposition, the sympathetic specificity and the sub-second latency claim. The wearable story is much weaker. A 2021 systematic review of consumer wrist-EDA devices (Ronca et al., Sensors) found only Empatica's E4 (predecessor to EmbracePlus) achieved research-acceptable agreement with lab-standard finger EDA in resting conditions, and none of the consumer-grade devices held up under free-living movement. Empatica has published cleaner comparison work under EmbracePlus, but the operationally useful literature — free-living, athlete population, sympathetic arousal vs training load separation — is essentially unwritten. The Garmin Cirqa, if it ships passive continuous wrist EDA in July, will be the largest natural experiment for the category to date; expect first-year validation attempts inside 3–6 months of ship date.
